feuil·le·ton
(noun) a part of a European newspaper devoted to light literature, fiction, criticism, etc.
Web serials are little-known in the literary world but feature stories with incredibly deep worldbuilding and intricate storylines. Historically, feuilletons have been home to world-famous literary works (e.g. The Count of Monte Cristo, The Three Musketeers, and Great Expectations), which were originally published as serials. The modern-day continuation of serials hosted on the web, supported by creator platforms like Patreon, is allowing an entire new generation of writers to build worlds and create stories on their own terms, as opposed to traditional writers who are dependent on the approval and creative interests of publishing companies. This has produced a huge selection of daring and creative works, spanning genres and ideas that are not well represented in the literary cosmos of published books. As a longtime fan of the scene, here are some of my favorites:

  • Worm and Ward ↗, a hyper-realistic superhero story that makes The Boys / Marvel feel as shallow as DC. It follows the story of a teenager who wants to do good but through a series of twists and turns is forced to become a villain to do so. Amazing at exploring the psychology of superpowered individuals, and societal dynamics / interests that would need to exist in the world for typical superhero tropes to be realistic.

  • Twig ↗, a biopunk story that follows several orphans in a world where bioengineering is developed in the early 1800s. A surprisingly personal and introspective thriller in a very unique, alien setting.

  • Mother of Learning ↗, a progression fantasy following an academy student who is trapped in an endless time loop and has to figure out why he's there and how to escape. A mix of Groundhog Day + Harry Potter + Sherlock Holmes, this is a story with a delightful hard magic system that is somehow both deeply intricate and understandable. Details matter, and there's clues to understanding the situation everywhere — if you miss them once, well, repetition is the mother of learning!
