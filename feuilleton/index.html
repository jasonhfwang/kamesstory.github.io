I've always loved stories. My dad used to tell me he would toss books across the room just to force himself to stop reading, and that obsession definitely carried over to me. When I was young, I frequently lost days in novels, and I spent more hours reading lore than actually playing any of the games they belonged to.
But my biggest addiction of all was a different kind of storytelling â€” web serials. Even now, after 15 years of quietly reading them, it's still a niche, and mentioning them tends to get polite smiles and general confusion. But I can't shake the feeling that I'm spying the glint of something special, something hidden from the mainstream eye.
When people first hear "web serial", trashy fanfiction is what first comes to mind. Admittedly, there's some truth to the stereotypes. They say that most of the internet is garbage, and unfortunately a large portion of fanfiction does echo that trend. But I've also spent years reading stories with worlds that eclipse anything I've found on the bookshelves. In many ways, these stories are what dominate the creative spaces of my mind.
Serialized storytelling itself isn't new, of course. Anime, manga, and TV series have thrived on weekly episodes for decades. Web serials function in a similar way, releasing chapters regularly. Every week, I look forward to the days when a new chapter drops, just like millions of people wait for the next episode of their favorite shows.
What I love most is the freedom that web serials offer authors. It's obvious when you look at each story's diversity of form. Traditional publishing demands a finished manuscript, predictability, and cautious publishers hesitant to bet on creative risks. Online authors, though, can share their stories chapter-by-chapter, directly engaging readers as the narrative unfolds. There's no distribution cost, and no middleman that skews incentives.
This direct connection is what makes web serials work. Readers financially support authors through platforms like Patreon, subscribing for early chapters or special extras. Li Jin's concept of "100 True Fans," inspired by Kevin Kelly's famous "1,000 True Fans," shows just how compelling a small dedicated audience can be.
This frees authors from the editorial oversight of a publisher, giving them the freedom to fully implement their vision. My favorite stories create new sub-genres â€” they involve world dynamics based on meta-narratives, investigations into superhero psychology and political theory, or explore unique concepts like a Victorian-era biopunk thriller. Serializing the content leads to living communities that can make story choices and produce a kaleidoscope of speculation.
In a way, web serials remind me of classics like "The Count of Monte Cristo" or Dickens's classics, which were originally published in newspaper feuilletons, chapter-by-chapter. Today the internet enhances this tradition, adding interactivity and community discussion, and making storytelling dynamic and engaging.
I'm especially excited about the untapped possibilities. The web naturally supports interactive and personalized narratives, branching storylines, or side-stories accessible through hyperlinks. The best authors are already playing with these ideas, figuring out how to use them to broaden their worlds, and tease alternative perspectives.
